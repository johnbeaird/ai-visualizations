04

What the lines mean

Each colored line tracks one piece of economic data. Here's what they are in plain English:

Main chart — the big economic picture
Fed Funds Rate
The interest rate the Federal Reserve sets. When it goes up, borrowing gets expensive. Think of it as the economy's "gas pedal" — high = brakes on, low = full speed.
CPI (Inflation)
How fast prices are rising. 2% is considered healthy. Above 5%? Your groceries and gas are getting noticeably more expensive.
10-Year Yield
The interest rate on a 10-year government loan. It drives mortgage rates and signals what investors think about the future.
Unemployment
Percentage of people who want a job but can't find one. Below 5% is strong; above 8% means trouble.
GDP Growth
How fast the whole economy is growing. Positive = expansion. Negative = recession (the economy is shrinking).
S&P 500
A basket of 500 big U.S. companies. Basically "the stock market." When it drops fast, people panic.
Bottom-left — Housing
30Y Mortgage
The interest rate on a typical 30-year home loan. Directly affects how much your monthly payment would be.
Case-Shiller
Home price index. When this goes up, houses are getting more expensive. The 2006 spike? That was the housing bubble.
Bottom-right — Sentiment (how people feel)
Sentiment
Consumer confidence — are regular people optimistic or worried about the economy?
PMI
Purchasing Managers Index — are businesses buying more stuff (growing) or less (shrinking)? Above 50 = growth.
💡
The faint red/pink vertical bands on the chart mark recessions — periods when the economy was shrinking. Notice how unemployment always spikes during these bands!

5 things to try right now

Open the terminal and try these — each takes under 2 minutes.
